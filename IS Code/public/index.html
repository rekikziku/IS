Purpose

This Independent Study aims to explore the use of Markov chain models for predicting population and migration patterns between states in the United States. This is done by using the 2019 population and state-to-state migration flow dataset from the United States Census Bureau. We develop Markov chain models to predict the population and probability of moving from one state to another for any given year and use these results to answer three main research questions:

Research Question 1

In a regular Markov chain model, the probability distribution of the states often reaches an equilibrium at some time n. Based on the population forecast, when do we expect the state-to-state migration probability to reach its equilibrium? Does the ranking of state by population remain constant until it reaches equilibrium?

Research Question 2

Based on the Markov chain population projection, which states have experienced the largest inflows and outflows of migrants? Does the prediction of the number of people entering and leaving each state remain constant or change over time?

Research Question 3

How accurate is the population forecast generated using Markov chain models? Assuming that it is not completely accurate, how far off is the forecast from the official projection done by the US Census Bureau and how much does the error vary over time?

Regular Markov Chains Overview

A Markov chain is a type of stochastic process that models a system that transitions between different states over time. In a Markov chain, the probability of transitioning from one state to another is determined by a set of transition probabilities, which are constant over time and depend only on the current state of the system.

Markov chains have numerous applications in various fields, such as physics, chemistry, economics, and computer science. They are particularly useful for modeling systems that exhibit memoryless behavior, meaning that the future behavior of the system is independent of its past behavior, given its current state.

    Equilibrium: A state in which the probability distribution of the Markov chain remains constant over time.

    Convergence Time: The amount of time it takes for the Markov chain to reach equilibrium.

    Transition Probability: The probability of moving from one state to another in a single step of the Markov chain.

    Population Distribution: The probability distribution of the Markov chain at a given time, which describes the likelihood of being in each state.

Although Markov chain models do not accurately predict population changes over time, our predictions are comparable to the actual population, where an increase in the predicted population indicates an increase in the actual population and vice versa.

Convergence Time: 178 years The convergence time was computed to the precision level where only whole numbers are considered. We expect the population of all states to be equal in the year 2191 with a population of 14,795,132.

There is a significant error that increases over time. Regardless of the error, there is a strong positive correlation between the actual and predicted population that decreases by about 0.03 for every 1-year increase. Overall, since the accuracy of the model decreases over time, we can assume that the predicted population will be less reliable as it converges to equilibrium.
